Construction Estimating

9 Construction Estimating Mistakes That Keep Contractors Busy but Broke

Learn how to catch scope, labor, overhead, margin, and cash-flow errors before submitting your next construction bid.

Draft previewPlanned publication: 2026-07-1416 min readPrepared by Mobi Estimates editorial team

Winning a job does not mean it was estimated correctly. A bid can be accepted because it is clear and competitive, or because it missed labor, scope, overhead, risk, or cash-flow pressure.

Learn how to catch scope, labor, overhead, margin, and cash-flow errors before submitting your next construction bid. This guide covers nine construction estimating mistakes and includes a summary table, worked example, diagnostic table, and pre-bid checklist you can adapt for your own process.

Note: This is construction-business education, not legal, tax, insurance, engineering, accounting, or licensing advice. Requirements vary by state, locality, project type, contract, trade, and worker classification. Verify requirements with qualified advisors and official sources.

1. Pricing from direct costs only

A common estimating shortcut is to add labor, materials, equipment, and subcontractors, then add “some extra.” That misses how construction companies actually absorb cost.

A general contractor’s estimate should separate cost layers so the number can be reviewed before it is sent.

Estimate layerWhat belongs thereWhy it matters
Direct costsLabor, materials, equipment, and subcontractors tied to the workThe visible cost of building the scope
Labor burdenPayroll taxes, workers’ compensation, benefits, and other labor-related cost where applicableWage rate alone usually understates labor cost
General conditions / project indirectsSupervision, temporary facilities, dumpsters, mobilization, cleanup, project admin, permits where applicableCosts required to run this specific project
Company overheadOffice, software, estimating time, trucks, accounting, management, insurance, shop, and adminCosts required to keep the company operating
Risk / contingencyDocument gaps, schedule pressure, escalation, access constraints, unknownsSeparates known uncertainty from profit
ProfitReturn for taking business riskNot the owner’s wage and not overhead

Procore’s construction estimating guide describes estimating as more than a takeoff: it includes reviewing the bid package, project conditions, pricing, indirect costs, risk, and final bid submission. CMAA’s construction estimating resource also treats estimating as a structured preconstruction process rather than a one-line price.

What to check: if the estimate has only labor, material, equipment, and subcontractors, it is probably incomplete. Add review lines for general conditions, overhead, risk, and profit.

2. Confusing markup and margin

Markup and margin are related, but they are not the same.

If estimated project cost is $100,000 and the contractor applies a 20% markup, the selling price is $120,000. Formula: $100,000 × 1.20 = $120,000. Gross profit is $20,000. Formula: $120,000 − $100,000 = $20,000. Gross margin is $20,000 ÷ $120,000 = 16.7%, not 20%.

Estimated costMarkupSelling priceGross profitGross margin
$100,00010%$110,000$10,0009.1%
$100,00020%$120,000$20,00016.7%
$100,00030%$130,000$30,00023.1%

That math matters when owners compare a target margin to the markup they add in the estimate. If the company wants a specific margin, the markup has to be calculated from the selling price target, not guessed from cost.

What to check: decide whether the company prices by markup or by target gross margin. Then make the formula consistent across estimates.

3. Writing a price before the scope is clear

A vague scope makes the estimate look cleaner than it really is. The problem shows up later in the form of unpaid extras, disputes, missed inclusions, or a proposal that the customer reads differently than the contractor intended.

Before pricing, confirm:

  • drawings, specifications, and bid instructions reviewed;
  • latest addenda included;
  • inclusions written clearly;
  • exclusions written clearly;
  • alternates and allowances separated;
  • assumptions listed;
  • owner, GC, subcontractor, and vendor responsibilities separated;
  • unclear items flagged for clarification.

For general contractors, this also means checking trade gaps. A drywall quote, flooring quote, electrical quote, and GC general-conditions line can each look fine on their own while still leaving gaps between scopes.

What to check: if the proposal cannot explain what is included and excluded in plain language, the price is not ready.

4. Trusting takeoff quantities without review

A quantity takeoff is an input, not the final answer. It can be wrong because of a scale issue, missed sheet, revised addendum, wrong unit, double count, or missing waste factor.

Common takeoff checks:

  • confirm plan scale and sheet version;
  • confirm latest addenda and revised drawings;
  • spot-check major quantities;
  • compare quantities against the scope narrative;
  • review waste factors and production assumptions;
  • separate base bid, alternates, and unit-price items;
  • confirm whether quantities are net, gross, or rounded for procurement.

CMAA’s estimating material emphasizes the importance of estimate basis, assumptions, and documentation. The practical point for contractors: a number is more useful when another person can trace where it came from.

What to check: pick the highest-dollar or highest-risk quantities and independently verify them before submission.

5. Using raw labor rates instead of loaded labor cost

The hourly wage is not always the full cost of labor. Depending on the company, location, worker classification, contract, and project, labor cost may include payroll taxes, workers’ compensation, benefits, insurance, supervision, travel, mobilization, nonproductive time, and safety requirements.

The IRS notes that worker classification depends on the facts of the relationship, and OSHA identifies worker safety rights and protections that employers must understand. This article does not interpret those rules for your company; it only flags that labor cost should not be treated casually.

What to check: use loaded labor rates approved by the company’s accounting or payroll process. If rates are provisional, label them provisional and replace them when better data is available.

6. Missing indirect costs, overhead, insurance, bonds, permits, and admin time

Some costs are not tied neatly to one line item, but they still have to be recovered.

Examples include:

  • estimator and owner time;
  • bid follow-up and revisions;
  • project management and supervision;
  • mobilization, cleanup, dumpsters, temporary protection, and site logistics;
  • trucks, tools, software, shop, and office cost;
  • accounting and administrative support;
  • insurance, bonds, permits, licensing, and compliance where applicable.

Bonding, insurance, permits, and licensing requirements vary by jurisdiction, project type, contract, owner, and trade. Do not assume one rule applies everywhere. Verify with official licensing boards, insurers, bond agents, contract documents, and qualified advisors.

The SBA’s finance guidance for small businesses emphasizes cash-flow management and financial records. Contractors need the same discipline in estimating: if overhead is not visible in the pricing model, it is easy to underrecover it.

What to check: maintain an overhead list and decide how it is recovered: labor-hour rate, markup, project allocation, monthly revenue target, or another company-approved method.

7. Treating every job as equally risky

Two jobs can have the same direct cost and very different risk.

A clean repeat project with known drawings, known subs, reasonable schedule, and familiar owner is different from a rushed bid with incomplete drawings, long payment terms, tight access, and unclear scope responsibility.

Review risk items before final pricing:

  • incomplete drawings or specifications;
  • aggressive schedule;
  • difficult access or logistics;
  • unfamiliar customer, architect, engineer, GC, or subcontractors;
  • unclear payment terms;
  • material escalation exposure;
  • unusual inspection, safety, or compliance requirements;
  • high coordination burden;
  • large upfront cash requirement.

What to check: decide whether risk belongs in contingency, clarification language, alternate pricing, schedule assumptions, or a no-bid decision. Do not bury all risk inside profit.

8. Ignoring cash flow and capacity

A job can be priced profitably and still create problems if the company cannot fund or manage it.

Before bidding or accepting a job, review:

  • how much labor, material, equipment, and subcontractor cost must be paid before payment arrives;
  • billing schedule, retention, deposits, progress payments, and stored-material rules;
  • supervision and PM bandwidth;
  • whether the job blocks better-fit work;
  • whether estimating, procurement, field, and admin teams can handle the timing.

SBA finance guidance highlights cash-flow planning as part of managing a business. In construction, payment timing can turn a good-looking estimate into a working-capital problem if it is not reviewed early.

What to check: add a cash-flow and capacity review to the go/no-go decision, especially on larger jobs or faster growth.

9. Submitting without a bid review checklist

A bid review is not bureaucracy. It is the last chance to catch mismatched documents, missing addenda, weak exclusions, bad formulas, uneven subcontractor scopes, and unclear risk before the number leaves the building.

A useful review asks:

  • Are we using the right documents?
  • Are the inclusions and exclusions clear?
  • Are the biggest quantities checked?
  • Are labor assumptions realistic?
  • Are subcontractor and vendor quotes leveled?
  • Are overhead, general conditions, risk, and profit intentional?
  • Are clarifications attached?
  • Is this the right job to win?

What to check: make the review checklist part of the estimating workflow, not a memory test five minutes before the deadline.

Worked example: small commercial tenant improvement estimate

This simplified example shows how markup, margin, overhead, and risk can be separated. It is educational only. Real pricing varies by company, location, contract, labor market, trade mix, project documents, risk, insurance, bonding, taxes, and accounting policy.

Example project: a small commercial tenant improvement with selective demolition, light framing/drywall, finishes, MEP subcontractor work, and normal business-hours coordination.

Calculation formulas used below:

  • Labor burden: base labor wages × 28% = $42,000 × 0.28 = $11,760.
  • Direct cost subtotal: base labor + labor burden + materials + equipment + subcontractors = $42,000 + $11,760 + $58,400 + $6,500 + $73,250 = $191,910.
  • Cost before company overhead: direct cost subtotal + general conditions = $191,910 + $18,500 = $210,410.
  • Company overhead allocation: cost before company overhead × 8% = $210,410 × 0.08 = $16,833, rounded to the nearest dollar.
  • Contingency/risk allowance: (cost before company overhead + company overhead allocation) × 5% = ($210,410 + $16,833) × 0.05 = $11,362, rounded to the nearest dollar.
  • Total estimated cost: cost before company overhead + company overhead + contingency = $210,410 + $16,833 + $11,362 = $238,605.
  • Markup: total estimated cost × 20% = $238,605 × 0.20 = $47,721.
  • Final selling price: total estimated cost + markup = $238,605 + $47,721 = $286,326.
  • Gross profit: final selling price − total estimated cost = $286,326 − $238,605 = $47,721.
  • Actual gross margin: gross profit ÷ final selling price = $47,721 ÷ $286,326 = 16.7%.

The contractor applied a 20% markup, but the actual gross margin is 16.7%. That is not a problem if the contractor intended it. It is a problem if the contractor thought the job carried a 20% margin.

Pre-bid review checklist

This checklist is intentionally detailed. It can be turned into a one-page downloadable tool later.

Documents and scope

  • Plans and specifications reviewed.
  • Latest addenda confirmed.
  • Bid instructions, due date, alternates, allowances, and unit prices reviewed.
  • Scope inclusions reviewed.
  • Scope exclusions clearly written.
  • Clarifications and assumptions attached.
  • Owner/GC/customer responsibilities separated from contractor responsibilities.

Quantities, labor, subs, and vendors

  • Takeoff independently checked for scale, sheet version, and major quantities.
  • Waste factors and rounding rules reviewed.
  • Subcontractor bids leveled against the same scope.
  • Vendor quotes checked for date, inclusions, exclusions, freight, tax treatment, and lead time.
  • Labor production assumptions reviewed.
  • Labor burden included where applicable.
  • Equipment costs included.

Indirect costs, risk, and business requirements

  • General conditions included.
  • Schedule and supervision included.
  • Permits, bonds, insurance, safety, licensing, and compliance considered where applicable.
  • Company overhead accounted for.
  • Escalation and project risk considered.
  • Contingency justified rather than used as a hidden catch-all.
  • Markup converted to actual margin.
  • Payment timing and cash-flow needs reviewed.

Final review

  • Proposal language matches the estimate scope.
  • Major formulas checked.
  • Major subcontractor and vendor assumptions checked.
  • Final bid reviewed by the owner, estimator, PM, or another responsible person before submission.
  • Go/no-go decision confirmed: this is a job the company actually wants to win.

FAQ

What is the most common construction estimating mistake?

The most common pattern is incomplete cost structure: the estimate includes visible direct costs but misses scope, labor burden, overhead, risk, or margin math. The exact mistake varies by company, which is why a checklist and closeout review are useful.

How do contractors avoid underbidding?

Contractors reduce underbidding risk by reviewing scope, takeoff quantities, labor production, subcontractor quotes, general conditions, overhead, risk, cash flow, and margin before submission. Historical job-cost data should be used when available.

What is the difference between markup and margin in construction?

Markup is added to cost. Margin is gross profit divided by selling price. In the simple example above, a 20% markup on $100,000 creates a $120,000 selling price, $20,000 gross profit, and a 16.7% gross margin.

Should every estimate include overhead and profit?

A construction company needs a pricing method that recovers overhead and produces profit over time. How overhead and profit are calculated varies by company, project type, contract, market, accounting method, and risk profile.

Do licensing, bonding, insurance, tax, and labor rules apply the same everywhere?

No. Requirements vary by jurisdiction, trade, worker classification, contract type, project type, and owner requirements. Verify with official agencies, insurers, bond agents, accountants, attorneys, and other qualified advisors before relying on an estimate.

Can software fix estimating mistakes?

Software can help organize information, standardize workflows, reduce repetitive work, and make review easier. It does not replace contractor judgment, accurate source data, clear scope, qualified compliance advice, or disciplined bid review.
