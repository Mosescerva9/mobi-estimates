How to Estimate Labor Costs in Construction

A practical workflow for estimating construction labor hours, crew assumptions, and labor-cost checks without relying on guesswork.

Draft preview8 min readPrepared by Mobi Estimates editorial team

Labor is one of the easiest parts of a construction estimate to understate because the cost depends on both hours and conditions. A useful labor estimate should show the crew assumption, production assumption, rate basis, and risk notes—not just one lump-sum number.

Quick answer: estimate labor by breaking the scope into tasks, estimating crew-hours for each task, applying the correct labor-rate basis, and checking the result against job conditions before adding markup or margin.

Note: This article is educational estimating workflow guidance. It does not provide wage benchmarks, tax advice, worker-classification guidance, legal advice, or a universal labor-burden percentage.

The basic labor-cost formula

Use this simple structure before adding company-specific details:

  • Crew-hours = crew size × hours on task.
  • Direct labor cost = crew-hours × hourly rate basis.
  • Labor estimate = direct labor cost + documented company-specific labor costs, if applicable.

The key is not the formula alone. The key is documenting the assumptions behind every number.

Common labor estimating mistakes

MistakeWhat it looks likeSafer estimating habit
Estimating from memory“This usually takes two days” with no crew size or task splitWrite task, crew, hours, and production assumption
Mixing ratesWage, loaded labor, subcontractor cost, and billing rate get treated as the same numberLabel the rate basis in the estimate
Ignoring conditionsAccess, phasing, occupied work, or cleanup is not reflectedAdd a condition note before bid review
Hiding labor in one lineA single labor allowance covers multiple activitiesSplit labor by task or phase
Skipping estimate-to-actual reviewThe same labor miss repeats on future jobsCompare estimated crew-hours to actual crew-hours after completion

Pre-bid labor review checklist

  • Every labor-heavy task has a visible task name.
  • Crew size is documented.
  • Crew-hours are shown or easy to reproduce.
  • Rate basis is labeled.
  • Unusual site conditions are noted.
  • Cleanup, mobilization, layout, supervision, and punch work are considered where applicable.
  • Subcontractor labor and self-performed labor are not mixed without explanation.
  • The final labor number can be reviewed by someone who did not build the estimate.

Where markup and margin fit

Labor cost is not the same thing as selling price. Once the labor cost is estimated, the company still needs to decide how overhead, markup, and margin are handled. For pricing math, read Construction Markup vs. Margin.

FAQ

What is the simplest construction labor-cost formula?

A simple starting point is crew-hours × hourly rate basis. The estimate should also document what the rate basis includes and what it excludes.

Should labor burden be included?

That depends on the company’s accounting policy and estimate structure. If burden is included, label it clearly. If it is excluded, make sure it is accounted for somewhere else before pricing the job.

Why do labor estimates miss so often?

Common reasons include vague task definitions, undocumented crew assumptions, production rates copied from a different job, and failure to adjust for site conditions.
