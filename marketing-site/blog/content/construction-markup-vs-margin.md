# Construction Markup vs. Margin: How to Price Jobs Correctly

A contractor can add a markup to cost and still miss the gross margin the company expected. That is because **markup** and **margin** measure different things.

**Quick answer:** markup is added to cost. Gross margin is gross profit divided by selling price. A 20% markup on a $100,000 estimated cost creates a $120,000 selling price, $20,000 gross profit, and a 16.7% gross margin.

> **Note:** The examples below are educational pricing math, not a universal pricing rule. Real pricing varies by company, location, project risk, contract terms, labor market, overhead structure, tax treatment, insurance, bonding, and accounting policy.

## Markup vs. margin in one table

| Term | Formula | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|
| Markup | Gross profit ÷ cost | How much profit is added on top of estimated cost |
| Gross margin | Gross profit ÷ selling price | What percentage of the final selling price remains as gross profit |
| Selling price | Cost + markup dollars | The price submitted to the customer |
| Gross profit | Selling price − cost | Dollars left before other below-the-line expenses, depending on accounting method |

## Why 20% markup is not 20% margin

If estimated cost is **$100,000** and markup is **20%**, the markup dollars are **$20,000**.

Formula:

- Markup dollars = $100,000 × 0.20 = $20,000.
- Selling price = $100,000 + $20,000 = $120,000.
- Gross margin = $20,000 ÷ $120,000 = 16.7%.

The markup is measured against cost. The margin is measured against selling price. Because selling price is larger than cost, the margin percentage is lower than the markup percentage.

## How to price from a target margin

If a contractor wants a target gross margin, the formula works differently.

**Selling price = cost ÷ (1 − target margin)**

Example: estimated cost is **$100,000** and the company wants a **25% gross margin**.

- Selling price = $100,000 ÷ (1 − 0.25)
- Selling price = $100,000 ÷ 0.75
- Selling price = $133,333
- Gross profit = $133,333 − $100,000 = $33,333
- Gross margin = $33,333 ÷ $133,333 = 25.0%

That price is higher than a 25% markup. A 25% markup on $100,000 would create a $125,000 price and a 20.0% gross margin.

## Worked example: choosing the right pricing method

Assume a contractor estimates a small project at **$238,605** before markup or profit.

| Pricing method | Formula | Selling price | Gross profit | Gross margin |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 20% markup | $238,605 × 1.20 | $286,326 | $47,721 | 16.7% |
| 20% target margin | $238,605 ÷ 0.80 | $298,256 | $59,651 | 20.0% |
| 25% target margin | $238,605 ÷ 0.75 | $318,140 | $79,535 | 25.0% |

The right method depends on how the company sets price, recovers overhead, measures job profitability, and competes in its market. The important part is to stop mixing the formulas accidentally.

## Where overhead and profit fit

Contractors handle overhead differently. Some companies allocate overhead into labor rates. Others apply an overhead percentage, monthly revenue target, project allocation, or separate line in the pricing model. Some use markup to recover both overhead and profit; others separate overhead, contingency, and profit before final review.

The article should not tell every contractor one universal percentage. Instead, the estimating process should make the assumptions visible:

- direct labor, material, equipment, and subcontractor costs;
- labor burden;
- general conditions;
- company overhead;
- contingency or risk allowance;
- profit target;
- final selling price and actual margin.

## Common markup and margin mistakes

- Treating 20% markup as 20% margin.
- Adding markup before all costs are included.
- Forgetting labor burden, general conditions, or overhead.
- Using the same markup on low-risk repeat work and high-risk unclear work.
- Comparing bids without checking whether competitors included the same scope.
- Changing markup without reviewing closeout data and cash flow.

## Practical pricing checklist

- [ ] Estimated cost includes labor, material, equipment, and subcontractors.
- [ ] Labor burden is included where applicable.
- [ ] General conditions are included.
- [ ] Company overhead recovery method is clear.
- [ ] Contingency or risk allowance is justified.
- [ ] Markup and target margin are not mixed.
- [ ] Selling price, gross profit, and gross margin are reviewed before submission.
- [ ] The company understands why the selected margin is appropriate for this job.

## FAQ

### Is markup the same as profit margin?

No. Markup is based on cost. Gross margin is based on selling price.

### What markup gives a 20% gross margin?

A 25% markup produces a 20% gross margin when all else is equal. Example: $100,000 cost × 1.25 = $125,000 price. Gross profit is $25,000, and $25,000 ÷ $125,000 = 20%.

### Should contractors price by markup or target margin?

Either method can work if it is consistent and tied to the company’s cost structure, overhead, risk, and closeout data. The mistake is using one term while calculating the other.
